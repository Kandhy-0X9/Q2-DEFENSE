Eagles are large, powerful birds of prey known for their incredible eyesight, hooked beaks, and strong talons, symbolizing strength and freedom globally, with over 60 species primarily in Eurasia, Africa, and North America, hunting fish, mammals, and reptiles, building massive nests (eyries) in tall trees or cliffs, and often mating for life, like the iconic Bald Eagle.

Key Characteristics
    Appearance: Heavy heads, large hooked beaks, muscular legs with powerful talons. Feather colors vary but often include browns, whites, or blacks, with iconic white heads and tails on mature Bald Eagles.
    Size: Varies by species; Bald Eagles can have 7-8 foot wingspans, while the Philippine Eagle is the longest, and the White-tailed Eagle has the largest wingspan.
    Eyesight: Exceptionally sharp, allowing them to spot prey from great distances.

Behavior & Diet
    Hunting: Apex predators that catch prey with talons, often without landing, and use their beaks to tear flesh.
    Diet: Carnivores, eating fish, birds, snakes, rodents, and even carrion; some species are specialized (e.g., fish-eaters).
    Nesting: Build huge nests (eyries) in tall trees or cliffs, often returning to and adding to them yearly.
    Social: Monogamous and mate for life, using the same nest year after year. 

Species & Habitat
    Global Presence: Found on every continent except Antarctica, with the most species in Eurasia and Africa.
    Types: Includes Sea Eagles (like the Bald Eagle), Booted Eagles (like the Golden Eagle), Forest Eagles, and Serpent Eagles.
    Habitat: Diverse, from mountains and forests to grasslands and near water.

Cultural Symbolism
    Symbol: The national emblem of the United States.
    Appearance: Brown body with a distinctive white head and tail that develops at maturity (4-5 years).
    Status: Once endangered but has recovered significantly due to protection.
