Blackbirds are a diverse group of songbirds, famously the European Common Blackbird (a thrush with a melodious song, males all black with yellow bill, females brown) and North American blackbirds (icterids like Red-winged, Brewer's, Rusty, Tricolored), known for various plumages, habitats (gardens, marshes), and sometimes striking features like red shoulder patches, with symbolic meanings tied to mystery, change, and spirituality across cultures. 

Common Blackbird (Turdus merula) - Europe, Asia, introduced to Australia/NZ
    Appearance: Males are all black with a bright yellow bill and eye-ring; females and juveniles are dark brown with spots.
    Song: Famous for its rich, liquid, melodic song.
    Diet: Omnivorous, eating insects, worms, fruits, and berries.
    Habitat: Gardens, woods, hedgerows, and farmland. 

North American Blackbirds (Icterids)
    Red-winged Blackbird: Males have distinctive red and yellow shoulder patches, known for polygynous mating.
    Brewer's Blackbird & Rusty Blackbird: Other common all-black species in North America, belonging to the same family (Icteridae) as orioles and meadowlarks.
    Tricolored Blackbird: A rarer, noisy species forming large colonies in marshes, declining due to habitat loss.

Key Distinctions & Fun Facts
    Family: European Blackbirds are thrushes (Turdidae), while North American blackbirds are icterids (Icteridae).
    Mythology/Symbolism: Often linked to transformation, change, the spirit world, and divine messages.
    The Beatles' Song: The "blackbird" in the famous song is a Common Blackbird, but the actual recording features a different thrush species.
